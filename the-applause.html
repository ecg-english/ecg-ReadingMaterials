The Applause

Fiction

Original Text ▼

Hiroto was a comedian who murdered silence. Not with jokes, but with awkwardness. He was twenty-six, broke, and standing on the stage of a basement theater in Shimokitazawa. He told a joke about convenience store bentos. The audience stared at him. A man in the front row checked his watch. That silent gesture was louder than any booing.


After the show, dragging his feet through the rain-slicked alleys, Hiroto remembered a rumor he had heard from a drunk senior comedian.


"There is a small, abandoned shrine behind the vending machines on 3rd Street. It belongs to a forgotten god of entertainment. If you give it your everything, it gives you the crowd."


Hiroto found it. It was barely a shrine—just a rotting wooden box slightly larger than a doghouse, covered in moss and surrounded by empty beer cans. There was no majesty, only a damp, heavy silence.


He stood before it, soaking wet. He didn't have money to offer. He only had his desperate, pathetic life.


He clasped his hands.


"I don't care about dignity," Hiroto whispered to the rotting wood. "I don't care about respect. I just want to be funny. Take my seriousness. Take my sadness. Make my entire existence a joke."


He waited.


No demon appeared. No thunder crashed.


Only a stray cat meowed in the distance, and the wind rustled the dead leaves.


Hiroto felt foolish. He bowed once and turned to leave. He tripped over a loose paving stone and face-planted into a puddle of muddy water.


From the balcony of the apartment above, a couple was smoking. They saw him fall.


And they laughed.


They didn't just chuckle; they howled. "Did you see that guy? The way he bounced!"


Hiroto sat up, mud dripping from his nose. He felt a sharp pain in his knee, but the couple's laughter echoed in the narrow alley like a standing ovation.


The contract had been signed in silence.


The change was absolute. The next day, Hiroto went to a convenience store. He dropped his change. The clerk burst out laughing. He tried to apologize, and the customers in line doubled over, gasping for air.


It wasn't that he became witty. It was that his suffering became hilarious. His panic, his clumsiness, his genuine distress—it all bypassed the empathy of others and struck their funny bone directly.


Hiroto became a star. TV shows, commercials, world tours. He was a genius of physical comedy. He read the weather forecast with a grim face, and the nation giggled. He cried about his mother's illness on a talk show, and the studio audience roared with delight, thinking it was a brilliant parody of grief.


One night, Hiroto sat in a fine restaurant with the woman he loved, Akari. He held her hand. He wanted to propose. He looked deep into her eyes, his heart pounding.


"Akari," he said, his voice trembling with genuine emotion. "I love you. Will you marry me?"


Akari froze. Her lips quivered. Then, she burst out laughing. She laughed so hard she choked on her wine. "Oh, Hiroto!" she wheezed, wiping a tear. "You're always on! That's the funniest proposal skit I've ever heard! You really had me going!"


Hiroto's face went pale. "I'm not joking," he whispered. "I'm serious."


"Stop it!" she cackled, clutching her stomach. "You're killing me! Oh god, you're a genius! The look on your face is priceless!"


She couldn't stop. The waiters were laughing. The other diners were laughing. Hiroto sat there, a diamond ring in his hand, drowning in a sea of joy that felt like acid. He realized then that he was completely alone. He was screaming in a glass box, and everyone outside thought he was dancing.


Three years later, Hiroto stood on the edge of a skyscraper in Shinjuku. The wind whipped his coat. He was tired. Tired of being a clown. Tired of his pain being consumed as entertainment.


A crowd gathered below. They pointed up. Phones were recording.


"Look! It's Hiroto!" someone shouted.


"Is he filming a movie?"


"He's going to do a stunt! This is going to be legendary!"


Hiroto looked down at the dots of people. Tears blurred his vision.


"I'm sorry," he whispered to the wind. "I just wanted to be heard."


He stepped off the ledge.


Gravity took him. He plummeted, a dark streak against the neon city. The impact was brutal. A sickening crunch echoed through the plaza as his body hit the pavement. His limbs twisted at unnatural angles. Blood pooled around his head.


For a moment, there was silence.


Then, one person started clapping. Then another. Then thousands.


The crowd erupted into a standing ovation.


"Bravo!" they screamed. "What a finale!" "Did you see how he landed? Hilarious!" "He really commits to the bit!"


As Hiroto's consciousness faded into darkness, the last thing he heard was not a prayer, but the thunderous, deafening sound of applause.

和訳 ▼

Hiroto was a comedian who murdered silence. Not with jokes, but with awkwardness.

ヒロトは沈黙を殺すコメディアンだった。ジョークではなく、気まずさで。

He was twenty-six, broke, and standing on the stage of a basement theater in Shimokitazawa.

彼は26歳で、一文無しで、下北沢の地下室劇場の舞台に立っていた。

He told a joke about convenience store bentos. The audience stared at him. A man in the front row checked his watch. That silent gesture was louder than any booing.

彼はコンビニ弁当についてのジョークを言った。観客は彼を見つめた。最前列の男性が時計を確認した。その沈黙の身振りはどんなブーイングよりも大きかった。

After the show, dragging his feet through the rain-slicked alleys, Hiroto remembered a rumor he had heard from a drunk senior comedian.

ショーの後、雨に濡れた路地を足を引きずりながら歩き、ヒロトは酔った先輩コメディアンから聞いた噂を思い出した。

"There is a small, abandoned shrine behind the vending machines on 3rd Street. It belongs to a forgotten god of entertainment. If you give it your everything, it gives you the crowd."

「3番街の自動販売機の後ろに、小さな、放棄された神社があります。それは忘れられたエンターテインメントの神のものです。あなたがすべてを与えれば、それはあなたに観客を与えます」

Hiroto found it. It was barely a shrine—just a rotting wooden box slightly larger than a doghouse, covered in moss and surrounded by empty beer cans.

ヒロトはそれを見つけた。それはほとんど神社ではなかった——ただの腐った木箱で、犬小屋より少し大きく、コケに覆われ、空のビール缶に囲まれていた。

There was no majesty, only a damp, heavy silence.

威厳はなく、ただ湿った、重い沈黙だけだった。

He stood before it, soaking wet. He didn't have money to offer. He only had his desperate, pathetic life.

彼はその前に立ち、ずぶ濡れだった。彼は捧げるお金がなかった。彼には絶望的で哀れな人生だけがあった。

He clasped his hands.

彼は手を合わせた。

"I don't care about dignity," Hiroto whispered to the rotting wood. "I don't care about respect. I just want to be funny. Take my seriousness. Take my sadness. Make my entire existence a joke."

「私は尊厳を気にしません」ヒロトは腐った木にささやいた。「私は尊敬を気にしません。ただ面白くなりたいだけです。私の真剣さを取ってください。私の悲しみを取ってください。私の存在全体をジョークにしてください」

He waited.

彼は待った。

No demon appeared. No thunder crashed.

悪魔は現れなかった。雷は鳴らなかった。

Only a stray cat meowed in the distance, and the wind rustled the dead leaves.

ただ遠くで野良猫が鳴き、風が枯れ葉をさらさらと鳴らしただけだった。

Hiroto felt foolish. He bowed once and turned to leave. He tripped over a loose paving stone and face-planted into a puddle of muddy water.

ヒロトは愚かだと感じた。彼は一度頭を下げ、去ろうとした。彼は緩んだ舗装石につまずき、泥水の水たまりに顔から落ちた。

From the balcony of the apartment above, a couple was smoking. They saw him fall.

上のアパートのバルコニーから、カップルがタバコを吸っていた。彼らは彼が転ぶのを見た。

And they laughed.

そして彼らは笑った。

They didn't just chuckle; they howled. "Did you see that guy? The way he bounced!"

彼らはただクスクス笑っただけでなく、大声で笑った。「あの男見た？跳ね返り方！」

Hiroto sat up, mud dripping from his nose. He felt a sharp pain in his knee, but the couple's laughter echoed in the narrow alley like a standing ovation.

ヒロトは起き上がり、鼻から泥が滴り落ちた。彼は膝に鋭い痛みを感じたが、カップルの笑い声は狭い路地にスタンディングオベーションのように響いた。

The contract had been signed in silence.

契約は沈黙の中で結ばれた。

The change was absolute. The next day, Hiroto went to a convenience store. He dropped his change. The clerk burst out laughing. He tried to apologize, and the customers in line doubled over, gasping for air.

変化は絶対的だった。翌日、ヒロトはコンビニに行った。彼は小銭を落とした。店員は爆笑した。彼は謝罪しようとしたが、列に並んでいる客は息を切らして身をよじった。

It wasn't that he became witty. It was that his suffering became hilarious. His panic, his clumsiness, his genuine distress—it all bypassed the empathy of others and struck their funny bone directly.

彼が機知に富んだようになったわけではなかった。彼の苦しみが面白くなったのだ。彼のパニック、不器用さ、真の苦悩——それはすべて他人の共感をバイパスし、直接彼らの笑いのツボを打った。

Hiroto became a star. TV shows, commercials, world tours. He was a genius of physical comedy. He read the weather forecast with a grim face, and the nation giggled. He cried about his mother's illness on a talk show, and the studio audience roared with delight, thinking it was a brilliant parody of grief.

ヒロトはスターになった。テレビ番組、コマーシャル、世界ツアー。彼は身体コメディの天才だった。彼は厳しい顔で天気予報を読み、国中がクスクス笑った。彼はトークショーで母親の病気について泣いたが、スタジオの観客はそれを悲しみの見事なパロディだと思って喜びの声を上げた。

One night, Hiroto sat in a fine restaurant with the woman he loved, Akari. He held her hand. He wanted to propose. He looked deep into her eyes, his heart pounding.

ある夜、ヒロトは愛する女性、アカリと高級レストランに座っていた。彼は彼女の手を握った。彼はプロポーズしたかった。彼は彼女の目を深く見つめ、心臓がドキドキしていた。

"Akari," he said, his voice trembling with genuine emotion. "I love you. Will you marry me?"

「アカリ」彼は言った。彼の声は真の感情で震えていた。「愛してる。結婚してくれる？」

Akari froze. Her lips quivered. Then, she burst out laughing. She laughed so hard she choked on her wine. "Oh, Hiroto!" she wheezed, wiping a tear. "You're always on! That's the funniest proposal skit I've ever heard! You really had me going!"

アカリは凍りついた。彼女の唇は震えた。それから、彼女は爆笑した。彼女は笑いすぎてワインをのどに詰まらせた。「ああ、ヒロト！」彼女は息を切らし、涙を拭いた。「あなたはいつも本気ね！これまで聞いた中で最も面白いプロポーズのスキットよ！本当に私を騙したわ！」

Hiroto's face went pale. "I'm not joking," he whispered. "I'm serious."

ヒロトの顔は青ざめた。「冗談じゃない」彼はささやいた。「真剣だ」

"Stop it!" she cackled, clutching her stomach. "You're killing me! Oh god, you're a genius! The look on your face is priceless!"

「やめて！」彼女は腹を抱えて笑い転げた。「死にそう！ああ、あなたは天才よ！その顔がたまらない！」

She couldn't stop. The waiters were laughing. The other diners were laughing. Hiroto sat there, a diamond ring in his hand, drowning in a sea of joy that felt like acid. He realized then that he was completely alone. He was screaming in a glass box, and everyone outside thought he was dancing.

彼女は止められなかった。ウェイターは笑っていた。他の客も笑っていた。ヒロトはそこに座り、ダイヤモンドの指輪を手に持ち、酸のように感じられる喜びの海に溺れていた。彼はその時、完全に一人であることに気づいた。彼はガラスの箱の中で叫んでいたが、外のすべての人は彼が踊っていると思っていた。

Three years later, Hiroto stood on the edge of a skyscraper in Shinjuku. The wind whipped his coat. He was tired. Tired of being a clown. Tired of his pain being consumed as entertainment.

3年後、ヒロトは新宿の超高層ビルの端に立っていた。風が彼のコートを打った。彼は疲れていた。道化師であることに疲れていた。彼の痛みがエンターテインメントとして消費されることに疲れていた。

A crowd gathered below. They pointed up. Phones were recording.

下に群衆が集まった。彼らは上を指差した。電話が録画していた。

"Look! It's Hiroto!" someone shouted.

「見て！ヒロトだ！」誰かが叫んだ。

"Is he filming a movie?"

「映画を撮っているの？」

"He's going to do a stunt! This is going to be legendary!"

「スタントをするつもりだ！これは伝説になる！」

Hiroto looked down at the dots of people. Tears blurred his vision.

ヒロトは下の点のような人々を見下ろした。涙が視界をぼやかした。

"I'm sorry," he whispered to the wind. "I just wanted to be heard."

「ごめんなさい」彼は風にささやいた。「ただ聞いてもらいたかっただけです」

He stepped off the ledge.

彼は縁から足を踏み出した。

Gravity took him. He plummeted, a dark streak against the neon city. The impact was brutal. A sickening crunch echoed through the plaza as his body hit the pavement. His limbs twisted at unnatural angles. Blood pooled around his head.

重力が彼を取った。彼は落下し、ネオン都市に対して暗い筋となった。衝撃は残酷だった。彼の体が舗装に当たったとき、気持ち悪いバリバリという音が広場に響いた。彼の手足は不自然な角度にねじれた。血が彼の頭の周りに溜まった。

For a moment, there was silence.

一瞬、沈黙があった。

Then, one person started clapping. Then another. Then thousands.

それから、一人が拍手を始めた。それからもう一人。それから数千人。

The crowd erupted into a standing ovation.

群衆はスタンディングオベーションに爆発した。

"Bravo!" they screamed. "What a finale!" "Did you see how he landed? Hilarious!" "He really commits to the bit!"

「ブラボー！」彼らは叫んだ。「なんというフィナーレだ！」「彼の着地の仕方見た？面白い！」「本当に本気でやるね！」

As Hiroto's consciousness faded into darkness, the last thing he heard was not a prayer, but the thunderous, deafening sound of applause.

ヒロトの意識が闇に消えていくとき、彼が最後に聞いたのは祈りではなく、雷のような、耳をつんざく拍手の音だった。

作品解説 ▼

テーマと背景

「The Applause」は、成功と孤独、笑いと悲しみの対比を描いた短編小説です。コメディアン・ヒロトの物語を通じて、エンターテインメント産業における人間性の喪失と、真実の感情が消費される現代社会の問題を問いかけています。

物語の構造

物語は3つの主要な段階で構成されています：

  • 失敗と願望：ヒロトは舞台で失敗し、神社で成功を願います。
  • 成功と孤立：願いが叶い、スターになりますが、真実の感情が理解されなくなります。
  • 絶望と皮肉：自殺の瞬間でさえ、観客はそれをエンターテインメントとして受け取ります。

重要なシンボル

神社：忘れられたエンターテインメントの神の神社は、成功への切実な願いと、その代償の重さを象徴しています。神社は威厳がなく、腐った木箱に過ぎませんが、ヒロトの願いは叶います。

契約：「The contract had been signed in silence」という表現は、ヒロトが無意識のうちに、自分の真実の感情を犠牲にして成功を得る契約を結んだことを示しています。

ガラスの箱：「He was screaming in a glass box, and everyone outside thought he was dancing」という比喩は、ヒロトの孤立と、周囲の誤解を強烈に表現しています。

文学的技法

アイロニー：物語全体が皮肉に満ちています。ヒロトは「面白くなりたい」と願いますが、その結果、真実の感情が理解されなくなります。プロポーズの場面では、真剣な感情が笑いとして受け取られ、最終的には自殺でさえエンターテインメントとして消費されます。

対比：「Not with jokes, but with awkwardness」という冒頭の対比は、物語全体のテーマを予告しています。ヒロトはジョークではなく、苦しみによって笑いを取るようになります。

反復：「Tired of being a clown. Tired of his pain being consumed as entertainment」という反復は、ヒロトの疲労と絶望を強調しています。

現代社会への示唆

この作品は、現代のエンターテインメント産業とソーシャルメディア文化を批判的に描いています。真実の苦しみや悲しみが、エンターテインメントとして消費され、個人の尊厳が失われる問題を指摘しています。ヒロトの物語は、成功の代償として人間性を失う危険性を警告しています。

結末の解釈

物語の結末は、ヒロトの死でさえ拍手で迎えられるという衝撃的な皮肉で終わります。これは、現代社会において、真実の感情や苦しみが完全に消費され、理解されなくなったことを示しています。ヒロトは「ただ聞いてもらいたかっただけ」という願いを抱いていましたが、その願いは決して叶うことはありませんでした。
